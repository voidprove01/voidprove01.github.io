Working Paper

Privacy Choices in Personalized Search Markets

Who gains and who loses from gaining control over their personal data? We study consumer’s incentives to give up their data in exchange for better personalization in a competitive search marketplace. The platform acts as an information intermediary, who personalizes consumer’s search environment at the consent of consumers. Con- sumers, heterogeneous in search costs, are facing the following trade-off: enabling cookies help them find better match, but it also shapes the degree of price competition among sellers which could be harmful to consumers. I show that no privacy for all consumers improves match efficiency, expands market demands, and despite its am- biguous effect on equilibrium price, a platform should enable cookies for everyone, while pooling everyone’s cost type to maximize consumer welfare. However, when leaving the choice to consumers, a separating equilibrium may arise, in which con- sumers with low search cost remain anonymous in order to separate themselves from high search cost consumers. Compared to the consumer optimal outcome, almost all consumers are worse off, suggesting that the prevalent consent-based approach to privacy regulation may lead to unintended welfare loss. [paper]

Selling a New Product to the Crowd: A Mechanism Design Approach

This paper studies the problem of a monopolist selling a common-value product to buyers who privately observe independent signals.The seller commits to a mechanism that elicits information from buyers, chooses what to disclose, and sets prices. We show that any mechanism involving private, buyer-specific communication can be replicated—and weakly improved—by a single public disclosure. Under Bayesian incentive compatibility, the optimal mechanism reduces to posted pricing with threshold allocation; a dominant-strategy implementation arises through leave-one-out transfer schemes. We also identify economically relevant settings in which coarse information disclosure can become strictly optimal despite the absence of capacity constraints.

Algorithms at the Gate: The Quality–Variety Trade-off under Algorithmic Content Curation

This paper develops a framework for how platforms design screening thresholds and algorithmic accuracy when sellers behave strategically. I show how these choices shape the trade-off between quality and variety, and how the platform’s ability to commit—and the intensity of internal and external competition—changes the optimal level of governance.

Consumer search, comparison frictions, and conversion timing in marketplace browsing(WIP)

Using clickstream data from a secondhand marketplace, I am studying how users sequentially discover items before purchase. A central question is whether narrowing into a cluster of very similar products helps users find what they want faster, or instead creates comparison frictions that delay conversion. The project combines behavioral modeling, simulation, and marketplace intuition to analyze search depth, stopping decisions, and product discovery quality.

Other Projects

I'm passionate about building AI-assisted analytics and agentic workflows to accelerate insight generation and data-driven decision making. Below are a couple of projects that I've worked on.

Estimating long-term treatment effects under persistent interventions

Built a simulation framework to compare benchmark estimators for long-term treatment effects in settings with ongoing treatment exposure. Analyzed when surrogate-index methods underperform relative to sequential approaches, and how performance depends on treatment dynamics, proxy quality, and the relationship between short-term and long-term outcomes.

A behavioral model of relationship and opinion co-evolution on social networks(pre-doctoral work)

Develops an agent-based model to study the co-evolution of opinions and social ties in dynamic networks. Simulations on real-world social networks (including Facebook ego data) demonstrate that global consensus is robust to network structure at high tolerance levels, while low tolerance leads to endogenous polarization and social segregation—even in well-connected networks.

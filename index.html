Research

Privacy Choices in Personalized Search Markets

This paper examines consumers’ incentives to give up data in exchange for better personalization in a competitive search marketplace. The platform acts as an information intermediary, personalizing the consumer search environment with user consent. Consumers, heterogeneous in search costs, face a trade-off: enabling cookies helps them find better matches, but also shapes the degree of price competition among sellers in ways that may harm consumers. I show that a no-privacy regime can improve match efficiency, expand market demand, and, despite its ambiguous effect on equilibrium prices, a platform should enable cookies for everyone while pooling consumers’ cost types to maximize welfare. When privacy choice is left to consumers, however, a separating equilibrium may arise in which low-search-cost consumers remain anonymous to distinguish themselves from high-search-cost consumers. Compared with the consumer-optimal outcome, almost all consumers are worse off, suggesting that consent-based privacy regulation may generate unintended welfare losses.

Selling a New Product to the Crowd: A Mechanism Design Approach

This paper studies a monopolist selling a common-value product to buyers who privately observe independent signals. The seller commits to a mechanism that elicits information from buyers, chooses what to disclose, and sets prices. I show that any mechanism involving private, buyer-specific communication can be replicated—and weakly improved—by a single public disclosure. Under Bayesian incentive compatibility, the optimal mechanism reduces to posted pricing with threshold allocation; a dominant-strategy implementation arises through leave-one-out transfer schemes. I also identify economically relevant settings in which coarse information disclosure becomes strictly optimal despite the absence of capacity constraints.

Consumer Search, Comparison Frictions, and Conversion Timing in Marketplace Browsing (WIP)

Using clickstream data from a secondhand marketplace, I study how users sequentially discover items before purchase. A central question is whether narrowing into a cluster of very similar products helps users find what they want faster, or instead creates comparison frictions that delay conversion. The project combines behavioral modeling, simulation, and marketplace intuition to analyze search depth, stopping decisions, and product discovery quality.

Technical Projects

A selection of technical projects spanning causal inference, simulation-based evaluation, and AI-assisted analytical workflows.

LLM-assisted experiment diagnostics and reporting agent

Built a Python toolkit for automated diagnostics of large-scale online experiments, supporting SRM detection, covariate balance checks, treatment effect estimation, and bootstrap-based uncertainty quantification. Developed an LLM-assisted analysis agent that synthesizes experiment diagnostics, summarizes treatment effects, and generates structured investigation reports to accelerate experiment interpretation and data-driven decision making.

Estimating long-term treatment effects under persistent interventions

Built a simulation framework to compare benchmark estimators for long-term treatment effects in settings with ongoing treatment exposure. Analyzed when surrogate-index methods underperform relative to sequential approaches, and how performance depends on treatment dynamics, proxy quality, and the relationship between short-term and long-term outcomes.

The Dynamics of Belief and Relationship Co-evolution on Social Networks

This paper develops an agent-based model to study the co-evolution of opinions and social ties in dynamic networks. Simulations on real-world social networks (including Facebook ego data) demonstrate that global consensus is robust to network structure at high tolerance levels, while low tolerance leads to endogenous polarization and social segregation—even in well-connected networks.
